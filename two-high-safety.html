Are Two-High Safeties Ruining the NFL?

Jisung Lee
Interactive Data Analysis • Nov 2025

In recent years, NFL analysts have debated whether the rise of two-high safety coverages is fundamentally changing offensive football. This interactive analysis examines the data behind this narrative and reveals a more nuanced story about how defenses actually play post-snap.

Understanding Coverage Shells

Before diving into the data, let's clarify what we mean by coverage shells. Pre-snap, defenses show either one deep safety (Cover 1, Cover 3) or two deep safeties (Cover 2, Cover 4). But what matters most is what happens after the ball is snapped.

The Pre-Snap Trend

The narrative is partly true: defenses are showing two-high safety looks more frequently pre-snap. But as we'll see, this doesn't tell the whole story about what actually happens during the play.

Key Insight: While two-high pre-snap looks have increased from about 40% to 60% over the past decade, this is primarily a disguise tactic. The real story is in what defenses do after the snap.

The Art of the Rotation

Modern defenses excel at rotation. They show one look pre-snap and rotate to something completely different post-snap. This is where the "two-high safeties are ruining football" narrative breaks down.

Watch how safeties can rotate from a two-high look into single-high coverage, or vice versa. This pre-snap disguise is designed to confuse quarterbacks and make pre-snap reads more difficult.

What Defenses Actually Run

Here's where it gets interesting. Despite the increase in two-high pre-snap looks, the actual post-snap coverage distribution hasn't changed as dramatically as you might think.

Use the slider to explore different seasons. Notice how both one-high and two-high pre-snap looks lead to a variety of post-snap coverages. The flow between pre-snap and post-snap reveals the sophistication of modern defensive schemes.

Post-Snap Reality

When we look at what defenses actually run post-snap, the picture becomes clearer. The distribution of coverage types has remained relatively stable, even as pre-snap looks have evolved.

The Bottom Line: Defenses aren't necessarily running more two-high coverage post-snap—they're just getting better at disguising their intentions pre-snap. This makes the quarterback's job harder without fundamentally changing what defenses do after the ball is snapped.

Conclusion

So are two-high safeties ruining the NFL? The answer is more nuanced than the headline suggests. While defenses are showing two-high looks more frequently pre-snap, they're not necessarily running them post-snap at the same rate. What we're really seeing is an evolution in defensive disguise and complexity.

The challenge for offenses isn't just the coverage itself—it's the uncertainty. Modern defenses are better at hiding their intentions, making it harder for quarterbacks to make pre-snap reads and adjustments. That's the real story behind the "two-high" narrative.

"The best defenses don't just run good coverage—they make you think they're running something else." - Anonymous NFL Defensive Coordinator
